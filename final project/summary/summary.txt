This study utilizes the 2007 Youth Risk Behavior Surveillance System (YRBS) dataset to conduct a One-Way Analysis of Variance (One-Way ANOVA) and Tukey's HSD post-hoc test, examining the relationship between adolescent grade levels and weekly aerobic exercise frequency. After data preparation and the removal of missing or invalid survey responses, a highly representative sample of 11,631 valid student records was analyzed.The empirical results demonstrate that grade level exerts a highly significant statistical impact on weekly aerobic exercise frequency, with a p-value approaching zero (p < 0.001), providing overwhelming evidence to reject the null hypothesis. The data reveals a distinct, monotonic downward trend across the cohorts: freshmen (9th graders) maintain the highest frequency of physical engagement. However, physical activity drops significantly upon entering the 10th grade and reaches its lowest point during the senior year (12th grade). High school seniors average nearly half a day less exercise per week (approximately 0.49 days) compared to freshmen, and this specific contrast is statistically highly significant.
